Biological Pest Control

Biological control utilizes natural solutions to control agricultural pests in an environmentally safe manner. Biocontrol solutions are easily integrated into an Integrated Pest Management (IPM) program, which is a sustainable pest management strategy. When applied correctly, it can help growers limit their usage of synthetic chemicals, which can be extremely harmful to humans, wildlife, and soil health. At CABI, classical biological control has been shown to be an effective method for controlling invasive or non-native species by releasing biological control agents. (BioProtection Portal; Fanny Deiss, 2025)

Types of Biological Pest Control

  1. Importation (Classical Biological Control)
    • Introduces natural enemies to control pests in new environments.
    • Requires species that can adapt and sustain populations over time.
    • Early unregulated efforts sometimes caused unintended ecological issues.
  2. Augmentation
    • Enhances the population of natural enemies in a specific area.
    • Inoculative release: Small, periodic releases to establish long-term pest suppression.
    • Inundative release: Large-scale releases to rapidly reduce pest populations.
    • Effectiveness depends on interactions between pests and control agents.
  3. Conservation
    • Supports and protects existing natural enemies in the environment.
    • Can be achieved by planting nectar-producing crops to sustain beneficial insects.
    • Proven to reduce pesticide use and increase crop yields.

Biological Weed Control Agents

  1. Predators
    • Free-living organisms that consume multiple pests throughout their lifetime.
    • Commonly used for insect pest control in crops.
    • Example: Lady beetle larvae (active May–July) feed on mites, scale insects, and caterpillars.
  2. Parasitoids
    • Lay eggs on or inside the body of pests; larvae feed on the host, eventually killing it.
    • Often have a narrow host range, targeting specific pest species.
    • Examples:
      • Ichneumonid wasps – Target caterpillars.
      • Braconid wasps – Attack caterpillars and aphids.
      • Chalcid wasps – Parasitize eggs and larvae of various insect species.
  3. Pathogens
    • Microorganisms such as fungi, bacteria, and viruses that infect and weaken pests.
    • Can occur naturally or be applied as biological pesticides.
    • Example: The nuclear polyhedrosis virus (NPV) infects and deforms caterpillars, reducing their populations.

Advantages of Biological Control

  1. Targeted Action
    • Specifically targets pests, reducing harm to beneficial organisms.
    • Minimizes impact on non-target species and the surrounding ecosystem.
  2. Sustainability
    • Uses natural control methods, reducing environmental risks.
    • Suitable for long-term pest management in farms, gardens, and greenhouses.
  3. Cost-Effectiveness
    • Often cheaper than synthetic pesticides.
    • Reduces the need for repeated pesticide applications.
    • Classical biological control can provide a self-sustaining solution.
  4. Minimal Residue
    • Leaves little to no chemical residue on crops.
    • Meets food safety standards and supports organic farming.
  5. Safety
    • Lower risk of harmful side effects compared to chemical pesticides.
    • Reduces exposure to toxic substances for farmers and consumers.
  6. Resistance Management
    • Introduces new pest control mechanisms, slowing pesticide resistance.
    • Helps maintain the effectiveness of pest control strategies.
  7. Compatibility with Integrated Pest Management (IPM)
    • Easily integrates with cultural, mechanical, and chemical pest control methods.
    • Supports long-term, environmentally friendly pest management.
